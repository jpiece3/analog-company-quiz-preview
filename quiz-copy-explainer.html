Quiz Copy Explainer

The Analog Company - Strategic Breakdown of Copy Decisions

1. Question Rationale

+

Each of the 7 quiz questions serves a strategic purpose in understanding the prospect and routing them to the right product. Here's why each question exists.

card_selection

Question 1: "What does a typical day look like for you?"

Why This Question

This is the cornerstone question. Lifestyle context determines bag needs more than any other factor. A new parent has different requirements than a frequent traveler, even if both need "large capacity."

What It Reveals

  • Primary profile segment - This question directly maps to our 5 customer profiles
  • Emotional state - Someone "juggling it all" is stressed and needs relief; someone in "creative chaos" values self-expression
  • Urgency level - "New chapter" suggests recent life change and higher purchase intent

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • "Juggling it all" (15 pts) - High intent. They've identified a problem and are actively seeking solutions
  • "New chapter" (14 pts) - Life transition creates purchase urgency
  • "On the move" (13 pts) - Regular travel = regular bag usage = higher perceived value
  • "Creative chaos" (12 pts) - Lower urgency but strong brand affinity potential
scale_slider

Question 2: "How many items do you typically carry with you daily?"

Why This Question

Directly determines bag size recommendation. Mismatched capacity is the #1 reason for bag dissatisfaction. A slider forces honest self-assessment rather than aspirational answers.

What It Reveals

  • Capacity needs - Routes to Mini Tote, Medium Tote, or Totepack
  • Organization complexity - More items = more pockets needed
  • Price tolerance - Heavy carriers often willing to pay more for the right solution

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • 7-10+ items (16 pts) - Actively experiencing bag pain; current bag likely not working
  • 4-6 items (12 pts) - Moderate needs, may be satisfied or seeking upgrade
  • 0-3 items (8 pts) - Light carrier; may not need premium solution
card_selection

Question 3: "When it comes to bags, what matters most to you?"

Why This Question

Values alignment question. This determines messaging angle more than product selection. Someone who values "quality that lasts" needs different persuasion than someone who wants to "express myself."

What It Reveals

  • Decision criteria - What will seal the deal for this person?
  • Objection anticipation - Quality-focused = price objection likely handled; style-focused = may need more social proof
  • Email sequence tone - Drives copy angle in follow-up emails

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • "Works everywhere" (16 pts) - Clear problem, seeking specific solution
  • "Quality that lasts" (15 pts) - Investment mindset, willing to pay for right product
  • "Function first" (14 pts) - Practical buyer, features will convert them
  • "Express myself" (12 pts) - Emotional buyer, may need more nurturing
multiple_choice

Question 4: "Do you need your bag to carry a laptop?"

Why This Question

Critical feature filter. Laptop carry is a make-or-break requirement for many buyers. This question prevents recommending a Mini Tote to someone who needs a 17" laptop sleeve.

What It Reveals

  • Professional context - Large laptop = likely office worker or creative professional
  • Product routing - Eliminates certain products from consideration
  • Work-from-anywhere indicator - Laptop carriers often have hybrid lifestyles

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • "Yes, large" (14 pts) - Daily requirement, will value solution highly
  • "Yes, smaller" (12 pts) - Flexible needs, multiple products could work
  • "Sometimes" (10 pts) - Convertible/versatile options ideal
  • "No tech" (8 pts) - Lifestyle bags, style may trump function
multiple_choice

Question 5: "What's your biggest frustration with bags you've owned?"

Why This Question

Pain point identification. Buying is about solving problems. This question surfaces the specific problem we can address in result copy and email follow-ups. The "sigh" framing elicits honest, emotional responses.

What It Reveals

  • Primary pain point - Exact problem to address in messaging
  • Past purchase failures - What went wrong before
  • Feature priorities - Organization pain = pocket emphasis; style pain = customization emphasis

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • "Multiple bags" (16 pts) - Active problem, seeking consolidation
  • "No organization" (15 pts) - Daily frustration, high motivation to solve
  • "Fall apart" (14 pts) - Quality pain, likely replaced bags multiple times
  • "Look boring" (12 pts) - Desire-based, not pain-based (lower urgency)
yes_no_toggle

Question 6: "Are you ready to find your forever bag?"

Why This Question

Direct intent qualifier. The "forever bag" framing creates commitment bias. If they say yes, they've mentally committed to making a decision. This also segments tire-kickers from serious buyers for email sequencing.

What It Reveals

  • Purchase timeline - "Yes" = active buyer; "No" = needs nurturing
  • Email sequence routing - Hot path vs. cold nurture
  • Result page CTA intensity - Ready buyers get direct CTAs; browsers get softer approach

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • "Yes" (15 pts) - Self-identified as ready. High intent. Hot path candidate.
  • "No" (6 pts) - Honest about browsing. Needs education before purchase.
scale_slider

Question 7: "Where do you fall on the style spectrum?"

Why This Question

Style preference for product matching. The spectrum format (Classic vs Bold) avoids binary thinking and captures nuance. This determines whether we recommend neutral classics or limited edition colorways.

What It Reveals

  • Product color recommendations - Black vs Limited Edition colorways
  • Customization interest - Bold-leaning users are customization upsell candidates
  • Risk tolerance - Bold style = willing to try new things

Buyer Readiness Indicators

  • Bold (8-10): 14 pts - Engaged with style, emotional connection likely
  • Balanced (4-7): 12 pts - Practical, will consider multiple options
  • Classic (1-3): 10 pts - Risk-averse, wants proven options with social proof

3. Scoring Logic Explained

+

The scoring system translates quiz responses into a 0-100 "temperature" score that determines result page messaging and email sequence routing.

Score Distribution

Maximum possible score: 100 points. Minimum: 48 points. Each question contributes 6-16 points based on buying intent signals, not "correctness."

Temperature Band Thresholds

Hot 80-100 Points

80-100

Why 80? Statistically, users scoring 80+ have selected high-intent answers on at least 5 of 7 questions. They've self-identified as ready buyers with clear needs. Direct CTAs won't feel pushy to this segment.

Warm 50-79 Points

50-79

Why 50? This captures the middle majority. They have interest and some buying signals but need validation through social proof, comparisons, and education before committing.

Cold 0-49 Points

0-49

Why 49? Users below 50 consistently selected "exploring" answers. Hard sells will turn them off. Educational content, brand storytelling, and soft CTAs work better for this segment.

Factor Weights Rationale

Question Score Range Weight Why This Weight
Q1: Lifestyle 12-15 pts High Lifestyle determines urgency. "New chapter" = life event purchase trigger.
Q2: Carry Volume 8-16 pts High Heavy carriers experience more bag pain and value solutions more.
Q3: Priority 12-16 pts High "Works everywhere" = clear problem. "Quality" = investment mindset.
Q4: Laptop 8-14 pts Medium Laptop need is binary but doesn't indicate purchase readiness alone.
Q5: Frustration 12-16 pts High Pain points drive purchases. "Multiple bags" = acute problem.
Q6: Ready 6-15 pts Highest Direct intent declaration. 9-point swing is the largest single factor.
Q7: Style 10-14 pts Low Style preference affects product routing but not purchase intent.

4. Email Sequence Strategy

+

The 14-email, 5-sequence system is designed to match message intensity to buyer readiness while maintaining consistent brand voice.

Day/Timing Rationale

Welcome Sequence (Days 0, 2, 5)

Day 0: Immediate results delivery. Peak attention and excitement. Include discount code for immediate action.

Day 2: First follow-up after "sleeping on it." Address common objection (bag trade-offs) with solution (convertible design).

Day 5: Social proof deployment. By now they've either purchased or need external validation to proceed.

Cold Nurture (Days 1, 3, 7, 10)

Day 1: Acknowledge non-purchase without pressure. Validate their bag frustration.

Day 3: Educational content (features to look for). Position as helpful, not salesy.

Day 7: Brand story and values. Build emotional connection before asking for purchase.

Day 10: Soft check-in with FAQ format. Respect their timeline while keeping door open.

Warm Activation (Days 1, 3, 6)

Day 1: Comparison guide. They're ready to decide but need help choosing which product.

Day 3: Product-specific social proof. Reviews from people like them.

Day 6: Urgency push. Code expiration creates decision forcing function.

Hot Path (Days 1, 4)

Day 1: Direct action email. They're ready. Remove friction. State logistics clearly.

Day 4: Final urgency. Code expiration. Last chance before full price.

Win-Back (Days 1, 5)

Day 1: Re-engagement and feedback request. Understand why they didn't buy.

Day 5: Final offer (15% vs 10%). Higher discount as last resort before going silent.

How Quiz Answers Map to Segments

Tag Accumulation Method

Each answer adds tags to the user's profile. The segment with the most matching tags becomes the primary profile.

Example: A user who selects:

  • Q1: "Juggling it all" (+organized-multitasker, +high-capacity)
  • Q3: "Works everywhere" (+organized-multitasker, +versatility-priority)
  • Q5: "Multiple bags" (+versatility-pain, +traveler-adventurer)

Result: organized-multitasker wins with 2 direct tags + supporting tags

Tie-Breaker Rule

If two profiles tie, Question 1 (Lifestyle) wins. This question was designed to be the cornerstone identifier.
